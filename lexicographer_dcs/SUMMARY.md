## Interpretation
- An attested strict purely-lexicographic lemma is a **clean** refutation:
  MW cited *no* text, yet the word occurs in the corpus.
- **Strongest tier = bands 2-3** (rare/uncommon). These are typically plant,
  medical and technical terms MW had only from nighaṇṭus/kośas that DCS now
  attests (e.g. `SAlaparRI` Desmodium, `BfNgaja` Agallochum, `viqaNga`
  Embelia). Frequency 10-99 in dated texts is solid, non-accidental evidence.
- **Band 1 (hapax)** is weak: single corpus occurrences may be DCS lemmatizer
  artefacts. Spot-check, do not auto-apply.
- **Band 4-5 caveat: homograph collision.** A handful of top-band hits are
  short strings (`tA`="Lakṣmī") that collide with a high-frequency *grammatical*
  DCS lemma, not the MW sense. Top-band single/double-letter lemmas need manual
  disambiguation before use.
- **Method note:** a coarse "every `<ls>` is L." test over-counts: common words
  (`anta` "end", `anila` "wind") leave their main sense *uncited* and carry
  one minor L. subsense, so they look purely-lexicographic. The strict filter
  excludes any lemma with an uncited gloss or cross-ref stub, which removes
  these (broad 20,146 -> strict 18,930). This is the key methodological
  lesson for sense-level work in P3.
- This is **lemma-level** (lemma-now). It does not yet verify the specific
  *sense* MW hedged (sense-next needs sense-tagged corpus). For purely-lex
  lemmas the distinction collapses: MW had no witness for the word at all.
- Partially-hedged lemmas are deliberately excluded: there the lemma is
  already text-attested in other senses, so DCS presence cannot refute the
  specific `L.` sense without sense-level data.
