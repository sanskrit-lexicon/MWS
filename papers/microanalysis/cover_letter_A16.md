# Cover letter

Dear Editor,

I am pleased to submit for consideration in the *International Journal of Lexicography* a single consolidated paper, **"The microstructure of *Monier-Williams 1899*: a data-grounded framework, triangulated against three metalexicographic traditions"** (~7,400-word prose body, within IJL's 8,000–10,000-word window).

## Contribution

The paper builds a descriptive microstructural framework for *Monier-Williams 1899* (MW) **from the artefact outward** — treating MW's 18 formal blocks, 8 primary article types, and 3 orthogonal article-properties as the primary data, and consulting the three dominant metalexicographic traditions (Wiegand 1989, 2002; Hausmann 1977, 1985; Atkins & Rundell 2008) only afterward, as convergent witnesses rather than as the imposed lens. Five constructs emerge — *block, slot, profile, hedge, infrastructure* — each operationalised against the fully tagged CDSL digital edition (`mw.txt`, 286,561 records). The central empirical claim is that MW is **block-economical**: a six-block digital kernel (five in print) is reused across the whole dictionary with type-driven enrichment. Triangulation (§7) shows all three external traditions converge on the same three structural facts under different terminologies — kernel-plus-enrichment economy, the singular `<ls>L.</ls>` provenance hedge (40,212 instances, with a three-stage 1872→1891→1899 lineage), and the distinctive continuation entry — evidence that these are properties of the artefact, not of any one theory. Every numerical claim is reproducible from the published `mw.txt` via the scripts deposited in `analysis/`.

## Fit to venue

IJL is the natural home for this work on three counts. First, its methodological centre is the comparison of one grounded reading against three named metalexicographic traditions — squarely IJL's remit for framework work on actual dictionaries. Second, IJL's readership spans both European-tradition lexicographers (who will recognise Wiegand, Hausmann, and Atkins–Rundell) and historians of dictionary-making (who will care about MW's 1899-print / 21st-century-digital double identity). Third, the paper's typological-signature proposal — *block economy as a structural property of single-volume scholarly bilingual dictionaries* — extends naturally to other historical dictionaries in IJL's scope (Grimm's *Deutsches Wörterbuch*, the historical *OED*, the *Trésor de la langue française*).
